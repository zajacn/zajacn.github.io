<em>Atriophallophorus winterbourni</em> is a trematode parasite endemic to the South Island of New Zealand. It alternates between two hosts in its life cycle: <em>Potamopyrgus antipodarum</em> snail and waterfowl. The system is most famous for its host-parasite co-evolutionary interactions. In my projects I explored the the genomic signature of macro-and microevolutionary processes in the system.

      <h3>How does  <em>A. winterbourni</em> differ from other digenean trematodes </h3>
      <p>In this study we de novo sequenced and annotated genome of the parasitic trematode <em>Atriophallophorus winterbourni</em>. We then followed up with its comparison to other parasitic worms. </p>
      <p> - First, we reconstructed the species phylogeny, and dated the split of <em>A. winterbourni</em> from the Opisthorchiata suborder to approximately 237.4 Ma (±120.4 Myr). </p>
      <p> - We then addressed the question of which expanded gene families and gained genes are potentially involved in adaptation to parasitism. We used hierarchical orthologous groups to reconstruct three ancestral genomes on the phylogeny leading to <em>A. winterbourni</em> and performed a GO (Gene Ontology) enrichment analysis of the gene composition of each ancestral genome, allowing us to characterize the subsequent genomic changes. </p>
      <p>We found 13 gene families in A. winterbourni to have had more than ten genes arising through these recent duplications; all of which have functions potentially relating to host behavioral manipulation, host tissue penetration, and hiding from host immunity through antigen presentation. We identified several families with genes evolving under positive selection {% cite zajac2021gene %}
      </p>

      <h3>A Whole‐Genome Investigation of Mitonuclear Discordance in <em>A. winterbourni</em> </h3>
      <p>We previously reported mitonuclear discordance - discrepancy between mitochondrial and nuclear phylogenetic patterns - in <em>A. winterbourni</em> based on markers and attributed to vicariant isolation followed by postglacial recolonization {% cite feijen2022phylogeography %}.  </p>
      <p>Faster evolution of mitochondrial genomes relative to nuclear genomes creates selective pressure on nuclear genes involved in mitochondrial function to preserve mitonuclear compatibility required for energy production. That is because mitochondrial functioning depends on the expression of over a 1000 nuclear genes. </p>
      <p>In isolated populations, such coevolution occurs independently. Upon secondary contact, selection against mismatched mitonuclear combinations or for advantageous mitochondrial variants together with their co-adapted nuclear backgrounds can generate asymmetric gene flow between genomes, resulting in mitonuclear discordance.  </p>
      <p>We speculated that if those processes contributed to mitonuclear discordance in our system, nuclear-encoded mitochondrial genes would exhibit divergence concordant with the mitochondrial genome. </p>
      <p>We assembled the mitochondrial genome and then used high-coverage whole-genome pooled resequencing of parasites from five lakes across three geographic regions to characterize genome-wide patterns of divergence and test whether selection on mitonuclear interactions underlies this discordance.  </p>
      <p>We actually observed that loci with divergence patterns concordant with mtDNA (0.09% of the genome) were enriched for functions unrelated to mitochondria and potentially related to host–parasite interactions, suggesting host-driven adaptive nuclear introgression with mitochondrial co-segregation {% cite zajac2026whole %}
      </p>
    </td>
